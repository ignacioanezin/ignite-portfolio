The best camera for a mountain race is the one you're still willing to carry on
the third climb. Everything I pack gets weighed against a simple question: is the
footage this enables worth the energy it costs me to move it?

Most of the time the answer trims the kit down hard.

## Light beats capable, up to a point

A full-frame mirrorless body and two fast primes will out-shoot a bigger rig I'm
too tired to lift. I'd rather have a camera in my hand at the moment that matters
than the "better" camera buried in a pack I've stopped wanting to open.

Fast primes over a zoom: they're lighter, they hold up in the low light of early
starts and high cols, and the discipline of a fixed focal length makes me move
my feet instead of the barrel.

## Mount it, don't carry it

For the inside-the-effort shots, the camera lives on me — a chest or helmet rig —
not in my hands. That frees me to actually run, and it puts the lens exactly
where the experience is. A compact gimbal comes out for the moving landscape, and
goes away the moment it stops earning its weight.

## Self-sufficient or it doesn't happen

Power, audio, and storage for multi-day shoots, all of it packable. Out there
"I'll grab that on the way back" isn't a plan — there is no way back until it's
done. The kit has to be complete enough that the only limit on the footage is
where the athletes can go.

Run-and-gun isn't a compromise on quality. Done right, it's the only way to be
standing in the one spot where the real film is.
