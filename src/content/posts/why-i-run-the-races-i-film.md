There's a moment in every mountain race that a film crew on the sidelines will
never catch. It's not the finish line. It's somewhere around two-thirds in, on a
climb nobody photographs, when a runner stops pretending they're fine and the
real race — the one happening inside them — finally shows on their face.

To film that, you have to be there. Not parked at the road crossing with a long
lens. *There* — breathing the same thin air, moving at the same pace, close
enough that the camera stops being a camera and becomes just another thing on
the trail.

## The access is the work

Most sports content is made from the outside looking in. Drones, a crew at the
accessible checkpoints, a highlight reel cut to music. It's competent and it's
forgettable, because it never gets past the surface of the effort.

Being a runner first changes the math. I can carry a light kit up the same
approach the athletes take, catch the genuine weather window instead of
recreating it the next clear morning, and reach the exposed ridgeline that a
vehicle and a telephoto simply can't. The access isn't a bonus feature of how I
work — it *is* the work.

## Restraint over coverage

Embedded doesn't mean filming everything. It means being fit enough to be in the
right place, and quiet enough not to ruin the moment once you're there. The best
footage usually happens when the athlete has forgotten I exist.

That's the whole pitch, really. I run the races I film so the film remembers
what the race actually felt like — not what it looked like from the car park.
